Trust the Package

Here's something they don't teach in WSET: producers put better products in better packages.

That embossed label? The heavier glass? The elegant box? Those aren't accidents. When a winery invests in premium packaging, they're protecting a premium product.

Guest: "These two Cabernets are the same price. Which one?"
You: "This one has better presentation‚Äîyou can see it in the bottle weight and the label quality. That usually means the winery put more care into what's inside too."

You're not guessing. You're reading signals that the industry deliberately puts there. A $25 bottle in premium packaging often outperforms a $28 bottle in basic dress.

Sometimes $3 buys you a lot.

Always Ask the Budget

This is the most important question you'll ask all December:

"How much are you looking to spend?"

Not "What's your budget?" (too formal). Not "How much can you afford?" (too presumptuous). Just: How much are you looking to spend?

Why This Matters

Without a number, you're shooting in the dark. Show them a $50 bottle when they wanted to spend $20? Awkward. Show them a $18 bottle when they were ready to spend $40? You just left money on the table.

Guest: "I need a good red wine for a dinner party."
You: "Sure! How much are you looking to spend?"
Guest: "Maybe around $25-30?"
You: "Perfect. Let me show you a couple of options that would be great for that."

Now you're not guessing. You're not over-reaching or under-delivering. You're working within their comfort zone and maximizing value within that range.

üí° The Range Response

When they give you a range ($25-30), aim for the higher end. If they say "around $25," show them something at $28. People usually give themselves a little cushion. Respect the boundary, but don't undersell.
